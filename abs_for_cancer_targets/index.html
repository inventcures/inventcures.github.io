A Quick Primer: What Are Antibodies, Anyway?

Before we dive into the AI revolution, let's talk about what we're actually designing.

Antibodies are Y-shaped proteins your immune system makes to fight infections. Each antibody is exquisitely specific—it recognizes one target (an "antigen") and grabs onto it like a lock-and-key. Your body can generate billions of different antibodies, each with a unique binding site, which is why you can fight off such a diverse range of pathogens.

Antibody structure. The Y-shaped protein has two arms (Fab regions) that bind targets, and a stem (Fc region) that signals the immune system. The CDRs at the tips are where specificity comes from—and where AI design focuses.

Pharma companies figured out they could turn antibodies into drugs. Instead of fighting infections, therapeutic antibodies can:

  • Block cancer growth signals (Herceptin for HER2+ breast cancer)
  • Unleash the immune system on tumors (Keytruda, worth $30 billion in 2024)
  • Neutralize inflammatory molecules (Humira for autoimmune disease)

The problem? Making these antibodies has been stuck in the 1970s.

The Old Way: Mice, Prayers, and Luck

Here's the traditional process: You inject a mouse with your target protein. Wait weeks for its immune system to respond. Harvest B cells from its spleen. Fuse them with immortal cancer cells to create "hybridomas." Screen thousands of these colonies. Hope one produces an antibody that binds where you want it to.

This takes 12–24 months. Most campaigns fail.

The New Way: Design from First Principles

What if you could skip the mice entirely?

That's what "de novo" antibody design promises. Instead of evolving antibodies through biological selection, you compute them. You specify exactly where on the target you want to bind (the "epitope"), and software generates protein sequences that should fold into antibodies hitting that exact spot.

Until recently, this was science fiction. Now it's not.

The $200 Billion Shakeup

The antibody business has a dirty secret.

Behind the $200 billion market, behind the gleaming biologics facilities and the billion-dollar acquisitions, there's a process that would embarrass a medieval alchemist. Inject mice, wait, hope. That's basically it.

Four groups just demonstrated something different.

Using generative AI—the same class of technology behind ChatGPT and Midjourney—they've shown you can design antibodies de novo. From scratch. No mice, no phage display, no hybridomas. You specify where on the target you want to bind, and the software generates sequences that fold into antibodies capable of hitting that spot with nanomolar affinity.

The Four Contenders

JAM-2 "The Pragmatist"

Nabla Bio's JAM-2 has the numbers that make drug hunters pay attention: a 39% hit rate for VHH-Fc antibodies, 18% for full mAbs, tested across 923+ designs on 26+ targets. More importantly, 57% of their designs pass all developability filters on the first try—thermostability, monomericity, low polyreactivity—without any optimization.

The architecture is zero-shot, meaning the model doesn't need to be retrained for each new target. Designs are genuinely novel: over 95% have RMSD >10 Å to the closest known structure. They've hit GPCRs (CXCR4 at 1.4 nM), checkpoint inhibitors (PD-L1), and receptor tyrosine kinases (TrkA at <100 pM).

Chai-2 "The Precision Instrument"

Chai Discovery's Chai-2 achieves something almost unreasonable: structural accuracy below 1.7 Å RMSD. Their cryo-EM validations show interface RMSDs as low as 0.54 Å—essentially atomic-level prediction of where the antibody contacts the antigen.

Their KRAS G12V antibody binds at 1.5 nM and discriminates between G12V and wild-type and G12D—a single amino acid difference. But the headline is the GPCR agonist: an antibody against CXCR4 that doesn't just bind—it activates the receptor (EC50 = 164 nM). That's never been done computationally.

Origin-1 "The Pioneer"

AbSci's Origin-1 is built for a specific problem: targets where no structural precedent exists. Their "zero-prior" epitope focus means they're designing antibodies for binding sites with ≤60% sequence identity to any protein with a known antibody complex.

Hit rates are lower (~4% per target), but the targets they're hitting are precisely the ones that fail with conventional methods. Cryo-EM validation on COL6A3 and AZGP1 confirms the structures are real.

RFAntibody "The Open-Source Idealist"

David Baker's lab has released RFAntibody under an MIT license, making it the only fully reproducible option. The hit rates are lower (~1-2%) and you need to generate 9,000+ designs per target, but the validation is rigorous.

For academic labs and biotechs that can't afford proprietary platforms, this is a genuine alternative. The trade-off is throughput—months of validation versus weeks for commercial platforms.

The Oncology Landscape: Where AI Meets Cancer

Cancer is where these platforms will prove their worth—or won't. The oncology antibody market represents over $80 billion annually, and the targets are as diverse as cancer itself.

Immune Checkpoints

PD-L1 (CD274): JAM-2 demonstrated low-nanomolar VHH binders against PD-L1. With 1,996 disease associations and 13 approved drugs (durvalumab, atezolizumab, avelumab), PD-L1 is as validated as targets get.

Receptor Tyrosine Kinases

VEGFR2: JAM-2 achieved 3.3 nM binding—the receptor that drives tumor angiogenesis across virtually every solid tumor type.

TrkA: JAM-2's sub-100 pM avid binding is remarkable. NTRK fusions drive cancers across tissue types—the basis for tumor-agnostic drugs like larotrectinib.

Oncology GPCRs — The New Frontier

This is where the platforms diverge most dramatically. GPCRs have been "undruggable" by antibodies for decades. Now they're not.

CXCR4: The metastasis receptor. JAM-2's 1.4 nM binder and Chai-2's 164 nM functional agonist represent two different approaches: pure affinity versus functional modulation.

CCR8: The Treg target. Expressed selectively on tumor-infiltrating regulatory T cells. Chai-2's 453 pM binder (50% hit rate) represents a potential first-in-class opportunity—zero approved drugs exist.

GPRC5D: The myeloma target. Chai-2's 48% hit rate and 189 nM affinity—against a target not in their training data—demonstrates generalization.

Neoepitopes / pMHC — Precision Immunotherapy

This is where cancer meets immunology at the molecular level. TCR-mimetic antibodies that recognize neoepitope-MHC complexes can distinguish cancer from healthy tissue with exquisite precision.

KRAS G12V: Chai-2's tour de force. The antibody binds at 1.5 nM and shows no detectable binding to wild-type KRAS or G12D—a single amino acid difference. This is the precision required for safe neoepitope targeting.

TP53 R175H: With 3,277 disease associations—more than any other target—TP53 represents the ultimate "undruggable" oncogene. Chai-2 targeted it.

The GPCR Moment

Why GPCRs Matter

G protein-coupled receptors are 34% of all approved drugs but almost all are targeted by small molecules, not antibodies. They're membrane-embedded, conformationally dynamic, and have tiny extracellular loops. Designing antibodies against them was considered basically impossible until now.

JAM-2 achieved 1.4 nM binding to CXCR4. But Chai-2's result is something else entirely. Their CXCR4 antibody is an agonist—it triggers downstream calcium signaling with an EC50 of 164 nM. To design an antibody that not only finds its target but also induces a specific functional response represents capability that simply didn't exist before.

The Caveats

Reality Check

  • No in vivo data. None of these platforms have published mouse PK, tumor xenografts, or any in vivo proof of concept.
  • Immunogenicity is untested. Novel CDR sequences may trigger anti-drug antibody responses. Clinical data is years away.
  • Manufacturing is unvalidated. Producing these at clinical scale in CHO cells hasn't been demonstrated.
  • Most aren't available. JAM-2, Chai-2, and Origin-1 are proprietary. RFAntibody requires 9,000+ designs per target.
  • Hit rate ≠ drug. Affinity maturation, epitope optimization, format engineering—most hard work is downstream.
We've solved the "can we generate binders" problem. We haven't solved the "will they work in humans" problem. That second problem takes 10-15 years and a billion dollars per drug, with or without AI.

So What?

For big pharma: Computational antibody design capabilities will be table stakes in three years. Build, buy, or partner—but don't ignore.

For biotechs: If your differentiation is "we can find antibodies to hard targets," that moat is eroding fast.

For VCs: Platform plays need sharper differentiation than "we use AI." What's the moat when RFAntibody is MIT-licensed?

For academics: RFAntibody exists. The barrier to computational antibody design just dropped to "can you run Python and validate 9,000 candidates."

Where This Goes

Eighteen months from now, I expect:

  • IND-enabling data from at least one platform
  • Narrowing performance gaps as competition intensifies
  • Integration with AI affinity maturation for end-to-end computational pipelines
  • Expansion to harder formats—bispecifics, ADCs, multi-specific engagers

I would not bet against the idea that computational antibody design becomes the default starting point for any new campaign within five years. The traditional 12-24 month discovery timeline—inject a mouse, wait, screen, hope—may soon look as quaint as searching the literature by hand.

The $200 billion antibody market isn't going away. But how we populate it is about to change fundamentally.
